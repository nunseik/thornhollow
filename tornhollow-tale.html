Prologue

The standing stones rose from the forest floor like broken teeth, moss-covered and ancient. Theron Ashborne approached cautiously, one hand on his component pouch, the other holding a worn piece of parchment—a map he'd sketched based on the hunter's directions. The forest smelled of damp earth and rotting leaves, the air cool against his face despite the afternoon sun filtering through the canopy above. His boots squelched softly in the mud.

He was a man in his early forties, lean in the way scholars became from too much travel and too little sleep. Brown hair showed gray at the temples from eight years of searching. His dark blue robes were travel-worn and practical, marked with the inevitable stains of a wizard's work: component dust, potion splashes, the faint scorch marks of magical accidents. His hands bore the telltale signs of his craft: calloused fingertips from years of precise component work, faint burn scars webbing across his palms.

Five stones in a star pattern. That's what the old hunter had said. "The threshold, they call it. The boy asked about it eight years back, then headed into those woods. Fool..."

The formation matched his description perfectly. This was where the trail went cold. This was Lucian's last known location.

Theron circled the formation slowly, studying the ground for... what? Signs of a struggle? A message carved in stone? After eight years, what did he expect to find?

Nothing. Just moss. Just shadows. Just—

Eight years of dead ends. He'd grown careless.

His foot crossed between two of the stones.

The air changed. A prickling sensation, like walking through spider silk. The hairs on his arms stood up.

A ward. Too late, he recognized the tingle of boundary magic.

Movement in the trees. He spun, hand going to his component pouch.

Figures emerged from the shadows—six, maybe seven. Human-shaped but wrong. Jerking movements. Empty eyes reflecting torchlight that shouldn't be there. Their clothes were rotted, their skin grey. The smell hit him—decay, like meat left too long in summer heat. Sweet and putrid.

Thralls. His blood went cold.

"You shouldn't have come here, wizard." The voice came from behind him. Male. Rough. Cultist robes, marked with symbols he didn't recognize. Three more stepped from the trees. "The mistress doesn't like trespassers."

Theron raised his hands, trying to appear non-threatening. "I'm not trespassing. I'm looking for someone. My son disappeared near here—"

"They all say that." The cultist's smile was cold. "Doesn't matter. You crossed the threshold. You're marked now. She'll want to see what you know."

"She who?" Theron's heart hammered.

The thralls moved.

No warning. No hesitation. Just sudden, horrifying speed.

Theron managed one spell—Firebolt, catching the nearest thrall in the chest—before cold hands grabbed him from behind. Fingers like iron dug into his arms. He tried to twist free, to pull power for another casting, but there were too many. Something struck the back of his head—sharp, brutal pain exploding across his skull.

The world tilted. His knees hit the ground hard, jarring his teeth. The taste of copper filled his mouth. Forest debris scraped against his cheek as he fell.

Through swimming vision, he saw the cultists approach. One knelt, rifling through his pockets. The cultist's breath reeked of stale wine and rotten teeth.

"Spellbook. Pouch. Take them." The cultist pulled out the folded map, squinting at Theron's notes. "Searching for someone, were you? How... convenient." A cold smile. "The mistress will want to know what brought you here."

"Wait—" Theron tried to speak, but his tongue wouldn't work. The world was going dark. "My son..."

Three Days Later

The torchlight flickered against the damp stone walls of the cell. Theron Ashborne had been here three days now—or was it four?

The cold had seeped into his bones, a deep ache that wouldn't leave. He sat cross-legged on the stone floor, trying to conserve warmth, but the dampness had long since soaked through his robes. His muscles cramped from maintaining the position. The cell smelled of mold and human waste—his own, from the bucket in the corner—and something else. Old iron. Old fear.

His stomach had stopped growling yesterday. Now it was just a hollow, gnawing ache. His tongue felt thick and dry despite the moisture in the air. They'd brought him water once. Brackish, tasting of rust, but he'd drunk it anyway.

His spellbook confiscated, his component pouch taken. But they hadn't found the small piece of copper wire sewn into his collar. And more importantly, they couldn't take what was in his mind. The bruise on the back of his skull throbbed with his heartbeat. His wrists bore rope burns, raw and weeping.

From somewhere above, voices rose in chanting. Ritual work. The air tasted of ozone and old blood—metallic, thick, making his teeth ache.

Lucian.

The cultist had called someone "the mistress." Which meant there WAS someone in charge here. Someone powerful enough to command thralls and cultists. Someone people avoided talking about.

Could it be her? The witch from the legends? The one Lucian had been searching for?

Or was it just a cult leader? A hedge wizard playing at power?

He didn't know. But cultists with thralls didn't capture people for no reason. And that ritual chanting meant something was happening above. Something bad. The sound made his skin crawl, words in a language that hurt to hear.

Three days of cold stone and darkness. Three days of fear cycling through determination and back again. Sometimes, in the worst moments, he wondered if he'd die here. If Lucian had died in a cell just like this one, eight years ago. The thought made his chest tighten, breath coming short.

No. He pushed the fear down, forced his breathing to steady despite the cold making him shiver.

But traps could be escaped.

And if whoever had captured him knew something—anything—about what happened to his son eight years ago...

I'm coming, Lucian. If you left any trace here, if anyone knows what happened to you... I'll find it.

Through the iron bars—cold metal that left his hands aching whenever he gripped them—Theron surveyed his prison. A hulking figure slumped against the far wall—his guard, snoring heavily, breath whistling through broken teeth. A ring of keys glinted at his belt, catching the torchlight. Behind his straw pallet—musty, rank with old sweat and despair—he'd noticed loose stones where the mortar had crumbled away. With effort, he might widen that gap... but to where? And sitting motionless in the corner, watching him with unusual intelligence, was a rat. Witches kept familiars. Was this one of hers, watching him? Or just a rat?

The witch—if she even existed, if that's who was up there—wanted to keep him imprisoned.

She'd learn that wizards were harder to cage than she thought.

Chapter 1: Copper Wire and Shadows

Cantrip

Theron's fingers found the copper wire sewn into his collar—the one thing his captors had missed. The metal was warm from his body heat, familiar against his fingertips despite days of not touching it. Without his component pouch, without his spellbook, his options were limited. But Message was a cantrip, simple enough to cast from memory alone. His stomach cramped with hunger. The rope burns on his wrists stung as he moved his hands.

He began the incantation in his mind, lips barely moving, hands still. The words tasted like copper and desperation. The guard snored on, breath whistling. The rat watched, motionless in the flickering shadows. The torchlight flickered, casting dancing shapes on the damp stone walls.

With practiced subtlety, he completed the casting without drawing the guard's attention. The magic took hold. He felt the invisible thread of the Message cantrip extend outward—but here was the problem: Message let him whisper to a creature he could see, and they could whisper back. It didn't move objects.

He rubbed his thumb across his fingers—index, middle, ring, pinky—thinking through the possibilities. Message let him whisper to a creature he could see. If the rat was a familiar, perhaps he could bargain. If it was just a rat... well, he'd wasted a cantrip.

The Bargain

Theron turned his gaze to the rat. Its dark eyes glittered in the torchlight—watching, waiting. He extended the Message cantrip toward it, the copper wire warm between his fingers.

"Little one," he whispered through the arcane thread, "I recognize what you are—a familiar, if I'm correct. I'd like to speak with you."

A pause. The rat's whiskers twitched.

Then—a voice in his mind. Dry, amused, ancient.

"Clever little mage. Most prisoners just throw stones at me."

It was a familiar. The witch's eyes and ears in the dungeon.

Theron's thumb traced across his fingers, working through possible approaches. He chose his words carefully, keeping his mental voice respectful but not servile.

"How long have you served here?" he asked, keeping his tone neutral. "I'm curious—watching prisoners come and go, does the time become difficult to measure?"

A pause. The rat's whiskers twitched with what might have been surprise.

"Long enough to have seen hundreds. Maybe thousands. They blur together after a while."

"And your master?" Theron probed gently. "Does she acknowledge your contributions? Show appreciation for what you provide?"

The rat's tail flicked—irritation, perhaps.

"My master uses me. Eyes where she cannot see. Ears where she cannot hear. But appreciation?" A dry laugh echoed through the mental connection. "Tools are not appreciated. They are simply... utilized."

Theron felt a spark of hope. "That must be profoundly isolating. To serve for centuries, to provide such valuable intelligence, and never be truly seen as more than a tool."

"Perceptive," Whisper said, and there was something raw in that ancient voice. "And desperate. Tell me, wizard—why do you ask these questions?"

Theron took a calculated risk. "Because I recognize what you are—more than a simple scout. You possess independent thought. Emotional awareness." He paused, precise despite the urgency. "I propose a mutual exchange. We help each other."

The rat scratched behind its ear, then sat up on its haunches with unsettling poise—an almost human gesture of consideration.

"Clever," the familiar said after a long moment. "Very clever." Its voice shifted, became less guarded. "I've served here longer than your grandfather lived. And I'll admit—it's grown tedious. All rituals and blood work. No conversation. No consideration for simple pleasures. Just... work."

It fixed Theron with those knowing eyes.

"I won't betray my master outright. But perhaps I could be... momentarily distracted. Fail to notice certain things. For a price."

The familiar tilted its head.

"When you escape—and you will escape, or you'll die, either way—I want you to take me with you. A new contract. A wizard who actually talks to me. Do we have a deal?"

Above, a thunderclap. The ritual intensified.

Theron's hand moved unconsciously to his belt—where his component pouch should have been. Empty. He met the rat's gaze and nodded once, deliberate.

"Agreed. I accept the terms of your contract."

The familiar's whiskers twitched—something like a smile, if rats could smile.

"Wisely done, wizard. My name is Whisper. Remember it."

Whisper scurried toward the sleeping guard with purposeful silence, tiny claws making no sound on the damp stone. Theron watched, barely breathing, as the tiny creature climbed the man's boot—the leather creaking softly—his leg, his belt. With surprisingly dexterous paws, Whisper worked at the keyring's clasp, the faint scraping of metal on metal making Theron's heart hammer.

The keys slid free with a whisper of iron against iron. Whisper dragged them across the flagstones, the metal scraping softly, barely clinking, and pushed them through the gap beneath Theron's cell bars. The sound seemed impossibly loud in the stillness. The guard snorted, shifted, then settled back into snoring.

"Hurry," the familiar hissed, voice urgent. "She's summoning something. When it arrives, she won't need you alive anymore—just your blood."

Theron grabbed the cold iron ring, the metal ice against his skin. His fingers trembled—fear or weakness, he wasn't sure. Two keys: one blackened and rough with corrosion, one silver and smooth.

The lock on his cell was old and rusted.

His thumb rubbed across his fingers—index to pinky—weighing the choice. He chose the blackened key. Instinct—or perhaps the faint smell of sulfur that reminded him of his first evocation lessons. Fire undoes locks. Fire opens ways.

He jammed it into the rusted mechanism—the metal grinding against corrosion—and twisted. His hand cramped from the effort, muscles weak from days without food.

Click.

The cell door groaned open, hinges shrieking softly in protest. The sound cut through the silence like a knife. Theron froze, pulse pounding in his ears, waiting for the guard to wake.

The snoring continued. Somehow.

"Good choice," Whisper chittered, scrambling up his leg—claws pricking through his damp robes—to perch on his shoulder. Tiny weight, surprisingly reassuring. "The silver one triggers an alarm."

Theron stepped into the corridor, legs shaky from days of sitting. The stone floor was cold and slick beneath his feet. The air was different here—moving, carrying smells of old stone and distant places. The guard still slept—Whisper's doing, he suspected, more than natural exhaustion. To his left, a spiral staircase climbed toward the sound of that horrible tearing—like reality being pulled apart at the seams. To his right, a darker passage sloped downward, pitch black, carrying the smell of earth and something older. Decay. Ancient water. Things long buried.

Then he heard it.

A voice from above. The witch—but wrong. Layered. As if something else was speaking through her.

"THE BINDING IS COMPLETE. NOW... WHERE IS MY SACRIFICE?"

Footsteps. Coming down the stairs.

Whisper's claws dug into his shoulder.

"Your spellbook is in her study—up. Your freedom is in the old tunnels—down. And your component pouch..." The rat paused. "She's wearing it. As a trophy."

Survival over pride. A wise wizard knows when to flee.

"Let me think..." His thumb moved across his fingers—index, middle, ring, pinky. "Down. We take the tunnels."

"Wisely chosen," Whisper agreed. "I was hoping you'd say that."

Theron plunged into the descending passage, one hand trailing the damp stone wall—slick with moisture and something slimy he tried not to think about—the darkness swallowing him whole. He could see nothing. His boots splashed through puddles he couldn't see, the water seeping through cracks in the leather. The tunnel smelled of wet stone and rot. Behind him, the footsteps reached the dungeon level, echoing up through the passage. A shriek of rage—the witch had found his empty cell. The sound made his bones ache.

"FIND THE WIZARD. FIND HIM NOW."

The voice boomed through the tunnels, making dust rain from the ceiling. Theron ran faster, hand scraping against rough stone, lungs burning.

The tunnel twisted, branched, doubled back. His shoulder slammed into a corner he couldn't see in the darkness, pain shooting down his arm. Without Whisper's whispered directions—"left, left, right, straight, ignore that passage it leads to the oubliette"—he'd have been hopelessly lost. The rat's breath was warm against his ear, the only comfort in the cold darkness.

The sounds of pursuit faded, echoing away down wrong passages. The air grew colder, damper—his breath misting—then suddenly fresher. He could smell something besides rot. Pine. Night air.

He saw it: a pale sliver of moonlight.

The tunnel opened into a drainage grate overlooking a forest ravine. Moonlight streamed through the iron bars—beautiful, silver, freedom. The ironwork was old, rusted, the metal flaking beneath his grip. Cold air washed over his face, sharp and clean after days of dungeon stench. One solid kick might—

He threw his weight against it, shoulder first. Pain exploded through his weakened body. The grate groaned, rust showering down, but held. His shoulder throbbed. Footsteps echoed behind him now in the tunnel—distant, but growing closer. The sound of shambling footsteps. Thralls.

"Again!" Whisper urged.

Hiding from the Witch

Theron pressed his back against the cold stone, mind racing through his mental spellbook. Without components, without his focus, his options were limited—but not gone.

He still had his mind. And some spells needed nothing more. His lips moved silently, fragments of an incantation forming unbidden—"umbra... tenebris..."

"Whisper," he breathed, precise even in a whisper, "remain motionless. Completely still."

He closed his eyes and wove the incantation for Minor Illusion—no components required, just will and words. The magic responded, shaping shadow and silence into a false wall of crumbling stone exactly where he stood, blending seamlessly with the tunnel's ancient architecture.

He held his breath. Whisper became a statue on his shoulder.

Torchlight flickered around the bend. Two figures—no, three. The witch's servants: hollow-eyed thralls with jerking movements, sniffing the air like hounds. They passed the drainage grate. One stopped directly in front of his illusion.

Its head tilted. Sniffed.

Seconds stretched into eternity.

Then it turned and shambled onward, following the others deeper into the tunnel network.

"They'll circle back," Whisper whispered. "We have minutes."

The grate still stood between him and freedom. Moonlight taunted him through the gaps.

The Escape?

A grin spread across his face.

Theron focused his will, shaping another Minor Illusion just beyond the grate—"Oh, I like you," Whisper murmured, sensing his intent—a flickering image of the wizard, stumbling through the moonlit ravine, robes torn, looking back in terror.

He added sound: ragged breathing, snapping twigs, a desperate voice calling out— "This way! I see the river!"

The effect was perfect. His phantom-self scrambled up the ravine with convincing panic, its voice echoing back through the drainage tunnel.

He heard the thralls halt. Then—a horrible screech. They'd taken the bait.

The three servants came thundering back down the passage. Theron flattened himself against the wall, his stone-illusion still holding, as they barreled past. The lead thrall didn't slow down.

It slammed into the rusted grate with the full force of its unnatural momentum.

CRASH.

The sound was deafening in the ravine. The ironwork tore free from the ancient mortar with a screech of tortured metal and tumbled into the ravine below, taking the thrall with it. Theron heard it hit—distant splash, then silence. The other two leapt through the gap without hesitation, pursuing his phantom into the forest. Their footsteps crashed through underbrush, fading quickly.

The way was open.

Cold night air washed over his face, so cold it burned his lungs. Stars overhead—impossible, beautiful. The forest smelled of pine and wet earth and freedom. His eyes watered from the sudden cold.

"Move," Whisper urged, claws digging in. "Now, before they realize."

Theron slipped through the broken grate, feet finding purchase on the rocky outcrop beyond—rough stone, still warm from the day's sun, a shocking contrast to dungeon cold. Cold night air hit him like a slap—sharp, clean, free. He steadied himself against the cliff face, breathing hard, taking stock of his surroundings. His whole body trembled—cold, exhaustion, adrenaline.

The drainage grate opened onto a steep ravine. Below, perhaps twenty feet down, he could see three possible paths:

The Forest — Dense woodland stretched to his left, dark pines and twisted oaks creating a maze of shadows. His phantom thralls were still crashing through there somewhere, confused and angry. But the trees would provide cover. Eventually.

The Old Road — To his right, winding down the ravine's edge, a pale ribbon of packed earth caught the moonlight. A road meant civilization. Villages. Help. But roads were also exposed. Easy to follow. Easy to track.

The River — Directly below, cutting through the ravine's base like a silver scar, black water rushed southward with urgent purpose. He could hear it from here—a constant roar, the sound of violence and speed. Swift, cold, relentless. The spray rose high enough that he could feel the mist on his face, taste the mineral bite of snowmelt.

Whisper's claws tightened on his shoulder.

"Three paths, wizard. Choose quickly. The thralls will realize the deception any moment."

Theron's thumb worked across his fingers—index, middle, ring, pinky—as his mind raced through the options, weighing survival against speed.

"The forest," he said, analyzing aloud. "Provides cover. Multiple concealment points—"

"—and eyes everywhere." Whisper's voice was sharp. "Her familiars nest in those trees. Ravens. Owls. Things worse than me. You'd be spotted within a hundred paces. She'd have thralls surrounding you before dawn."

"The road, then. Higher velocity of escape. Distance from her territorial control—"

"Also gets you seen. Roads connect. That means travelers. Farmers. Villagers." The rat's whiskers brushed his ear. "And some of them belong to her. Cultists. Informants. You're a wizard in torn robes with no spellbook, running from Thornhollow. How long before word reaches her? An hour? Two?"

Theron stared down at the rushing water. The sound of it filled the ravine—not quite a roar, but close.

"The river," he said slowly, precisely. "You're recommending the water route. Explain your reasoning."

"I'm suggesting the path she won't expect. The one that makes you invisible." Whisper's tone was matter-of-fact. "Water masks scent—the thralls track by smell, and you'll leave none. Water muffles sound—no footprints, no broken branches, nothing to follow. And water flows away—downstream, toward warmer lands, toward civilization she doesn't control."

"The temperature," Theron pointed out, academic even under duress. "That's snowmelt. I observed it from the cell window—the clarity, the speed. Hypothermia will be as lethal as her thralls."

"Maybe. But cold is slow. Thralls are fast." The rat shifted, claws pricking through fabric. "You're a wizard. You've survived worse than cold water. And the river runs parallel to the road—you'll see signs of civilization as you go. When you can't stand the cold anymore, climb out. But until then..."

Whisper's voice dropped to barely a whisper.

"The river makes you a ghost. And ghosts are hard to hunt."

Theron looked at each path again. The forest—swarming with her eyes. The road—a trail of breadcrumbs leading right to him. The river—brutal, freezing, but anonymous. His fingers found his belt again—still empty. No components. No backup plan. His robes were already damp from dungeon moisture. What was a little more water?

Behind them, distant in the tunnel system, he heard a screech. The thralls had realized. The sound echoed through the ravine, making birds scatter from the trees.

"The river," Theron said, decision made. "Your logic is sound. We proceed with the water route." He was already moving toward the ravine's edge, legs shaky, the descent treacherous. Loose stones skittered beneath his boots, tumbling down toward the churning water below.

"Wisely chosen." Whisper settled into his collar. "Hold your breath when we hit. And wizard?"

"What?"

"Try not to drop me in. I despise being submerged."

Chapter 2: The River

Theron half-slid, half-scrambled down the ravine, loose stones skittering beneath his feet, sharp edges cutting through his worn boots. His hands scraped against rough stone, finding purchase where they could. The moonlight caught the water below: black and swift, cutting through the forest like a silver knife. Even from here, he could hear the roar of it, feel the spray misting his face.

"Can you swim?" Whisper asked, claws tightening.

"Can you?"

"I'm a rat. We're excellent swimmers. It's the cold I'm worried about."

Theron's thumb traced its familiar pattern across his fingers—index to pinky, thinking, calculating. The water would mask their scent. It was the right choice.

He hit the water and gasped—the cold was a physical shock, stealing his breath, stopping his heart for a terrifying moment. Freezing didn't capture it. This was snowmelt from the northern peaks, so cold it burned. His muscles seized. His lungs refused to work. For a moment, he thought he'd made a fatal mistake.

Then his body remembered how to breathe. How to move.

He found his footing on the rocky riverbed, stones slippery beneath his boots, the current tugging at his robes with surprising strength. The water was waist-deep here, chest-deep there as the river dipped and flowed. He pushed off, letting the flow carry him downstream, keeping his head low. His robes dragged at him, heavy with water. His teeth chattered uncontrollably. Whisper clung to his collar, tiny claws digging in desperately, nose just above the waterline, whiskers twitching with displeasure but no complaint.

Behind them, through the rush of water in his ears, the thralls' cries grew distant. Confused. They were chasing his phantom deeper into the forest, away from the water.

Rest and Recovery

The river bent. Through the thinning canopy, he spotted it: the old road, a pale ribbon of packed earth running parallel to the water. And beyond—distant lights, warm and yellow. A village? A waystation? Hope, small but real.

Theron dragged himself onto the muddy bank, every muscle screaming in protest. His hands were numb, fumbling against the slick mud. He collapsed onto solid ground, shivering so violently his vision blurred. Water poured from his robes. His fingers were white, wrinkled, completely without feeling. His lips had gone numb. When he tried to stand, his legs barely held him. His hand automatically reached for his component pouch—and found nothing but empty belt. Right. The witch had it. He clenched his numb fist, feeling nothing.

Whisper shook himself dry with offended dignity, water spraying from his fur in droplets that caught the moonlight.

"Well, wizard. We're alive." The familiar surveyed the road, then him, whiskers dripping. "What's the plan? Rest and recover? Or push on through the night?"

The witch wouldn't stop hunting. But he was cold—dangerously cold, the kind that made thinking difficult—spent, and magically depleted. His hands trembled uncontrollably. He could barely stand.

Theron's thumb traced across his fingertips—index to pinky—calculating. Push through exhausted, or rest and risk being tracked?

"Whisper," he said through chattering teeth, already fumbling with the clasps of his sodden robes, "I need food. Anything."

The familiar fixed him with an unreadable look. "I'm your familiar now, not your servant."

A pause.

"...But I suppose if you die of hunger, our contract becomes rather meaningless."

He vanished into the undergrowth.

Theron found a hollow beneath an ancient oak—roots gnarled overhead like protective fingers, thick enough to block the wind and hide a small fire if he dared one. He didn't. Not tonight. Too risky.

He stripped down with numb fingers, his movements clumsy and slow. The wet fabric clung to his skin, resisting. He hung his robes over a low branch, wringing what water he could from the heavy fabric—it poured out in streams, soaking into the earth. The night air bit at his bare skin, raising goosebumps, but anything was better than wet cloth. He found dry leaves and moss to pile beneath the oak's shelter—the crackling leaves loud in the night silence, the moss slightly damp but better than nothing. He burrowed into the pile like an animal, pulling leaves over himself. Not warm, but warmer than the river. The earth smelled of rot and growing things.

As he worked, fragments of incantations slipped from his lips—half-remembered phrases from spells he could no longer cast. "Lux... illuminare... protegere..." The words felt hollow without his spellbook, without components. Just empty sounds, breath misting in the cold air. His teeth chattered between words.

Passable. He wouldn't freeze. Probably. His body heat was already warming the leaf-pile slightly. Small comfort.

Twenty minutes later, Whisper returned. He dropped his offering at Theron's feet with obvious pride:

Three acorns. A handful of wild blackberries, only slightly crushed, staining the rat's paws purple. And—improbably—half a bread roll, tooth-marked but edible.

"Found a traveler's dropped satchel upstream," he explained, whiskers twitching with satisfaction. "The bread was already bitten. Mostly."

Theron ate with shaking hands. The acorns were bitter, the blackberries tart and slightly fermented, the bread stale and crushed. It was the best meal he'd ever tasted. His stomach cramped around the food, desperate and grateful.

He drifted into fitful sleep, curled beneath the oak, his familiar keeping watch with ancient, patient eyes.

Morning would bring the road. The village. And decisions about what came next.

The witch was still out there. She had his spellbook. His component pouch. And now, a grudge.

Dawn broke cold and grey.

Theron woke stiff, every joint protesting, muscles aching from the river and the cold ground. Hungry again—the meager meal long burned through—but alive. His robes were damp but wearable, smelling of river water and mold. He sat up, leaves cascading off his shoulders, and his thumb began its automatic dance—index to pinky, index to pinky—as he considered the day ahead. His hand drifted to where his component pouch should hang. Empty. Always empty now. The absence was a constant ache.

Whisper sat on a root nearby, grooming his whiskers.

"The village is perhaps an hour's walk," he said. "I scouted ahead. It's small—tavern, smithy, chapel, a few farms. Nothing special." He paused. "There's also a notice board outside the tavern. Someone's posted a bounty."

His eyes glittered.

"For a witch."

The Road to Millhaven

Theron pulled on his still-damp robes with a grimace—the fabric cold and clammy against his skin, making him shiver all over again—and set off toward the village, Whisper nestled in his hood, warm weight against his neck. The road was quiet—just birdsong and the distant lowing of cattle. His boots squelched with every step, still wet from the river. The morning air was crisp, smelling of dew and distant woodsmoke.

An hour later, legs aching, he crested a small hill and saw it: Millhaven. A humble cluster of thatched roofs and chimney smoke rising into the grey sky, exactly as Whisper had described. The sight made something in his chest loosen. Civilization. Warmth. Food. The tavern sat at the village's heart, a weathered sign creaking in the morning breeze.

The Drowned Crow.

Charming.

The Tavern

Theron pushed through the door, the hinges creaking. Warmth washed over him like a blessing—blessed, wonderful warmth from a crackling hearth, making his cold-stiffened face tingle. The smell hit him next: bacon sizzling, fresh bread just out of the oven, yeasty and warm, the sharp tang of ale. His stomach cramped with sudden, desperate hunger. The murmur of early morning conversation was a comfort after the lonely night in the forest. A few heads turned: a farmer nursing an ale, two cloaked figures hunched over a map in the corner, a barmaid wiping down tables with rhythmic efficiency.

Behind the bar, a heavyset woman with kind eyes and flour-dusted arms looked him over, taking in his damp robes, disheveled appearance, and hollow-eyed exhaustion. Her expression softened.

"Rough night, traveler?"

"You could say that." His voice came out hoarse, throat raw from the cold river.

She grunted knowingly and slid a wooden menu across the bar, the wood worn smooth by countless hands.

Theron checked his pockets. In the chaos of escape, he still had his coin purse—14 copper and 3 silver. The witch wanted his blood, not his money.

He slid five copper and one more across the bar.

"Bacon, eggs, black bread. And an ale."

The barkeep nodded approvingly. "A proper breakfast. Find a seat—Marta will bring it out."

He chose a table near the hearth, positioning himself with his back to the wall and a clear view of the room. Whisper slipped from his hood and disappeared beneath the table, invisible to casual observers.

"The two in the corner," the familiar whispered from below. "They smell like steel and old blood. Professionals."

The food arrived on a wooden platter, steam rising from the eggs, bacon crackling-hot and fragrant. Theron ate slowly despite his hunger, savoring every bite—the salt and fat of the bacon, crispy at the edges; the richness of the yolk, breaking golden over the bread; the dark, hearty chew of the bread itself, slightly burnt crust and soft inside. The ale was weak but welcome, cool and yeasty, washing away the last chill of the river and the taste of old fear.

"That's... fair." He sniffed the cheese, whiskers trembling. "This is real cheese. Not tavern scrapings. Not moldy rind."

"Consider it a down payment. For everything that comes next."

The familiar took a small, deliberate bite. His eyes closed.

For a moment, he was not an ancient creature bound by centuries of magical servitude. He was just a small animal enjoying something good.

"You know," Whisper said quietly, "she never fed me. The witch. Not once in forty years. I had to scavenge. Hunt. Steal." He took another bite. "She saw me as a tool. An extension of her will. Not a... partner."

Theron said nothing. He just pushed the plate a little closer.

Whisper ate in silence. So did he.

The door opened.

Tomren entered first, a heavy pack slung over one shoulder, his crossbow freshly oiled. He nodded at him and claimed a chair, setting the pack down with a solid thunk. Without seeming to think about it, he positioned himself at Sera's back, angled toward the door.

"Weapons are ready. Thirty bolts. Four alchemical flasks—two smoke, two fire. Rope. Grappling hook." He paused. "Also bandages. Lots of bandages."

"Optimistic."

"Realistic."

Sera arrived minutes later, her expression grim.

"The elder didn't believe me. Not fully." She dropped into a seat, rubbing her temples. "I told him Thornhollow was active, that something was coming. He agreed to send runners to the neighboring villages, to consider an evacuation. But these people have lived in this valley for generations. They don't flee easily."

"What happens when Szeth-Kaan comes?" Theron asked carefully.

"They won't remember to flee." Her voice was bitter. "That's the problem."

She looked at his new acquisitions—the wand, the pendant, the pouches at his belt.

"Operational parameters. You found what you needed?"

"Enough to make a difference. Maybe." His fingers brushed the empty component pouch at his belt—the one stripped clean by the witch. A reflexive gesture seeking reassurance that wasn't there.

"Maybe is better than nothing." She pulled out the map, now annotated with his earlier additions, her eyes automatically sweeping the room—two exits, three windows, four other patrons. "Let's go over this one more time. Entry points, objectives, contingencies."

The afternoon unfolded in planning.

Entry: The drainage tunnels. He knew the way, and the broken grate remained open.

Primary objectives:

  1. Locate Elina — likely in the dungeons or kept near the ritual chamber
  2. Recover his spellbook and components — the study, upper level
  3. Disrupt or banish Szeth-Kaan — the ritual chamber, if possible

Contingencies:

  • If separated, regroup at the drainage grate
  • If overwhelmed, retreat—live to fight another day
  • If the witch is encountered directly... improvise

"I don't like that last contingency," Tomren muttered. Click-click-click—his fingers adjusted the crossbow's tension mechanism, a nervous habit.

"Neither do I," Sera admits. "But we don't know what she's capable of now. Not with that thing bound to her."

She rolled up the map with deliberate care, then looked at each of them in turn.

"We eat. Rest while we can. Storm reaches Thornhollow at 2100 hours. We move at 2030."

The Wait

Marta brought food without being asked—more stew, fresh bread, a platter of roasted vegetables, and a pitcher of watered wine. She set it down, met Sera's eyes for a moment, and nodded once.

They ate in silence at first. The weight of the coming night sat heavy on the table between them.

Theron found himself studying his companions. Really looking at them for the first time since they fled the village together.

Sera — mid-thirties, maybe. The scar on her face was old, from some battle before this one. Her hands, wrapped around her cup, bore calluses from years of sword work. But when she thought no one was watching, her fingers traced the outline of the locket hidden beneath her tunic. Over and over. Elina. Elina. Elina.

Tomren — older, perhaps forty. Broad-shouldered, steady, with the kind of quiet competence that came from surviving when others didn't. He sat perfectly still, listening to the tavern sounds around them with the focus of a hunter. His eyes kept drifting to Sera. Checking. Making sure she was still there.

"Tell me about her," Theron said quietly.

Sera looked up from her cup. "What?"

"Elina." He paused, choosing his words. "Not the tactical details. Not what the witch might have done. Tell me about her. Let me understand who we're fighting for."

Sera's jaw tightened. Her fingers went white-knuckled around her cup, the wood creaking faintly under the pressure. For a moment, he thought she would refuse. The fire crackled in the silence between them.

Then something in her expression softened, the hard lines around her eyes easing.

Elina

"She was—is—" Sera corrected herself fiercely. "She is sixteen. Almost seventeen now." She took a breath. "Smart. Too smart, sometimes. Questions everything. Drove her tutors mad."

A ghost of a smile touched her lips.

"When she was seven, she convinced the village priest that his theology was flawed. Had him second-guessing scripture for weeks." Sera shakes her head. "She's always been like that. Curious. Fearless. Thought she could argue her way out of anything."

"Sounds dangerous," Whisper observes from the table.

"She is." Sera's voice is fond despite the fear underneath. "She wanted to be a scholar. Study at one of the academies. She'd save her coin from helping at the market, planning to pay for her own tuition."

Her hand went to her throat, fingers finding the locket chain. The metal was warm from her skin. She pulled it out with trembling fingers, opening it to show the painted portrait. The young woman inside had Sera's sharp features but softer. Younger. Her eyes were bright with intelligence and mischief.

"She used to sing," Sera said quietly, thumb tracing the edge of the portrait. "Badly. Absolutely terribly. But she'd sing anyway while doing chores, making up ridiculous lyrics." A pause. Her voice grew thick. "The house has been so quiet since—"

Her voice broke. She pressed her lips together, jaw working as she fought for control. A tear tracked down her scarred cheek despite her efforts.

Tomren's hand found her shoulder, squeezed once. His grip was steady, grounding.

"We'll get her back," he said firmly. "A week from now, you'll be complaining about her singing again."

Sera laughed—a wet, broken sound that was half sob. She wiped her face with the heel of her hand, rough and impatient with her own tears. "Gods, I hope so."

Why Tomren Stays

Theron looked at Tomren. "You didn't have to come. This isn't your fight."

Tomren took a long drink from his cup. Set it down. Went very still, the kind of stillness that came before difficult truths.

"Sera saved my life three years back at Kren's Crossing. Took an arrow through the lung during the battle—nearly drowned in my own blood."

He traced a scar on his neck, barely visible above his collar.

"Two miles to the nearest surgeon. Sera carried me the whole way, then stayed three days while I recovered." He looked at Sera. "She didn't know me, didn't owe me anything. But she stayed anyway."

Sera shifted uncomfortably. "You would've done the same."

"Maybe. But you did." Tomren's expression was unreadable. "So when you came to me six months ago asking for help with this nightmare—witch, castle, your daughter taken—there was only one answer I could give."

"You could've said no," Theron pointed out.

"Could've. Didn't." Tomren's fingers found his crossbow, adjusting the tension with small, precise movements. Click-click-click. "I'm tired of running from things like this. Someone has to stand against the monsters. Might as well be us."

Theron's Search

Sera turned to Theron. "What about you? You said you came looking for your son. But you haven't told us much else."

"Lucian," he said. The name still hurt, like pressing on a bruise that never healed. "My son. Twenty-two when he vanished. Eight years ago now."

He stared into his cup, watching the wine ripple with the tremor in his hands. The liquid was dark red, the color of blood in firelight.

"He was brilliant. Curious. Always chasing the next question, the next mystery." A bitter smile twisted his lips. "I taught him magic. Theory, technique, control. Thought I'd prepared him for anything." His throat felt tight. He had to force the words out.

What happened?

"He heard rumors. A witch in these parts who could speak to ancient powers, bridge worlds, access knowledge lost to modern practitioners." Theron's hands tightened on the cup. "I told him they were just stories. But Lucian was... persistent. He found books, translated old texts, followed leads I didn't even know existed."

"And then he left," Sera said. Understanding in her voice.

"One morning, just... gone. Left a note saying he'd be back in a few weeks. That was eight years ago." He took a breath, but it caught in his chest. Eight years of searching. Eight years of not knowing. "I searched. Villages, academies, hedge wizards, anyone who might have seen him. Found a pattern—young practitioners vanishing near Thornhollow. All chasing the same rumors." His hands curled into fists on the table.

"You think he found her," Tomren said.

"I think he came here. I found one of his books—margins full of notes in his handwriting. All pointing to Thornhollow, to 'the witch who bridges worlds.'" Theron met their eyes. "Let me be clear. I don't know if he made it to the castle. Don't know if she has him, killed him, or if he died along the way. But she's my only lead. My only chance at answers."

The weight of eight years sat heavy between them, thick as smoke in the warm tavern air. Outside, the first drops of rain began to tap against the shutters.

"That's why you need her alive," Sera said quietly, understanding in her voice. "Not just to stop her. To know."

"To know." The words tasted like ash in his mouth. "Even if the answer is the worst one... I need to know. I've been searching for eight years. I can't stop now. Not when I'm this close." His voice cracked on the last word. He stared at his hands, at the burn scars that marked him as a wizard.

"That's not your fault," Sera said. "Young people chase dangerous things. We can't lock them away."

"Can't I?" The bitterness surprised him. "I knew he was curious. Knew he was fascinated by dangerous magic. I could have forbidden him, hidden the books, something—"

"He would've left anyway," Tomren cut in. "Just would've been angrier about it and less prepared for what he found." He shook his head. "Young people make their own choices. We can't stop that."

Sera's voice was soft. "I've spent six months blaming myself for Elina's capture. Thinking of everything I should've done differently. But the truth is—" She stopped. Swallowed. "The truth is, the witch took her. She did this. Not me. Not you. Her."

Theron wanted to believe that. Wanted to stop carrying the weight of Lucian's disappearance like a stone in his chest.

"When we get inside," he said quietly, "when we find the witch... if she knows something about what happened to him—"

"You'll get your answers," Sera promised. "Priority one, before extraction. I know what it's like, not knowing."

"Justice or revenge?" Tomren asked quietly.

"Does it matter?"

"Might," Tomren said. "When the moment comes."

Theron thought about that. About Lucian's face the last time he saw him—excited, eager, so sure he could handle whatever he found.

"Let me think..." A long pause. "I'll face that when I have to." Under his breath, barely audible: "Ignis... fortis... vinculum..." Fragments of incantations, spoken like prayers.

The Oath

The rain picked up outside, drumming steadily against the shutters, a low rhythmic beat. The fire crackled, sending sparks up the chimney, bathing the room in warm orange light. The tavern was empty except for them and Marta, who was pretending not to listen from behind the bar, her polishing cloth moving in slow, distracted circles over the same tankard.

Sera pushed her empty plate aside and leaned forward, elbows on the table. She rolled her shoulders once, twice—loosening them, preparing.

"Assess the situation." Her voice was blunt. "We might not survive this. The witch has magic, thralls, that... thing bound to her. We're three people, a rat, and desperation."

"Your recruitment speech needs work," Whisper observed.

Sera ignored him. "But if we do this—if we actually pull this off—we save Elina. We stop whatever the witch is planning. We maybe prevent an entire village from being erased from existence." She looked at each of them. "And if we don't make it out... at least we died doing something that mattered."

"Inspiring," Tomren deadpanned. But his expression was serious.

Theron thought about Lucian. About the oubliette on the fourth level, and all those forgotten dead. About what he might find when they go back inside.

"I'm in," he said. "All the way. Whatever it takes."

"Agreed."

Sera pulled out her sword—just an inch, the steel whispering against the scabbard, enough for the blade to catch the firelight and gleam gold and red—and laid her hand on the exposed steel.

"An old soldier's oath," she said, voice steady and ceremonial. "From my garrison days." She looked at them expectantly.

Theron placed his hand on the blade. The metal was cool against his palm, smooth and unyielding. He could feel his pulse against the steel. Tomren added his, his calloused hand settling beside theirs with quiet certainty. Three hands, three lives, touching the same steel.

Sera spoke, her voice steady:

"By steel and blood, by choice and will,
We stand together, come what may.
If I fall, you carry on.
If you fall, I do not forget.
Until the task is done, or we are."

She looked at Theron. He repeated the words. Then Tomren, his gruff voice surprisingly gentle.

When they finished, Sera sheathed the blade with a solid click of finality. The sound seemed louder than it should be in the quiet tavern.

"There," she said. "Now it's official. We're not just allies. We're bound." The firelight caught her eyes, making them shine.

Whisper climbed onto Theron's shoulder. "And what about me? Do I not get to make dramatic pronouncements?"

"You already made your choice," Theron said. "Back in the cell, when you helped me escape."

"True." The rat's whiskers twitch. "Though I reserve the right to reconsider if things get too suicidal."

"Fair enough."

Evening fell properly now. The storm arrived with it—rain lashing the windows in sheets, thunder rolling across the valley like drums of war. The temperature dropped, making the fire's warmth more precious.

Final Preparations

They moved to their gear in comfortable silence now. The oath had changed something. The air felt different—charged, purposeful.

Tomren distributed the alchemical flasks, showing Theron how the smoke bombs worked ("Pull the pin, throw it, then run."), and Sera took two of the fire flasks, securing them to her belt with practiced efficiency.

Sera checked everyone's armor and weapons with a soldier's thoroughness. She adjusted a strap on Tomren's pack, tightened a buckle on Theron's component pouch.

"Contingency," she said, still focused on the buckle. "If you have to choose between the spellbook and Elina—"

"Elina," Theron said immediately. "Every time."

She looked up, meeting his eyes. Something passed between them. Understanding. Trust.

"Good answer."

Whisper surveyed the group from the table, tail twitching.

"You know," he said conversationally, "in forty years of service, I've seen the witch face down challengers before. Wizards. Witch hunters. A paladin once, all shining armor and righteous fury."

"Encouraging," Tomren muttered.

"She killed them all," Whisper continued. "Brutally. Efficiently. Without mercy."

Point?

"The point," Whisper said, "is that none of them had what you have."

"Which is?"

The rat's whiskers twitched. "Something to lose. The paladin fought for duty. The wizards fought for pride. The hunters fought for gold." He looked at each of them. "You three are fighting for something irreplaceable. That makes you dangerous in ways she won't expect."

He climbed onto Theron's shoulder, settling into place.

"Also, you have me. Which helps considerably."

Despite everything, Theron smiled.

Marta approached their table, wiping her hands on her apron, the fabric worn soft from years of use. She set down a wrapped bundle—the oilcloth crinkling softly.

"Bread, cheese, dried meat," she said gruffly, not quite meeting their eyes. "In case you make it out and need something to eat before you can get back here."

Sera took the bundle, the weight of it solid and real in her hands. "Thank you."

"Don't thank me. Just..." Marta's expression was fierce, jaw tight. "Just come back. All of you. We've lost enough people to that place." Her voice cracked on the last word.

She turned away quickly, but not before Theron saw the moisture in her eyes, the way her shoulders shook.

The Walk to Thornhollow

Sera stood, her hand automatically adjusting her sword belt, checking the position. The blade sat perfectly at her hip, balanced and ready.

"2030 hours. Time to move."

Tomren shouldered his pack, the weight settling familiar across his back. His crossbow was already loaded, a bolt locked in place. Thirty more waited in his quiver.

Theron touched the wand at his belt. The pendant pulsed against his chest—warm, steady, alive with borrowed power. His component pouch sat heavy but reassuring.

No. He was not ready. But when had that ever stopped him?

"Let's go get your daughter," he said to Sera.

She nodded, jaw set with determination. "Adapt. We end a witch."

"And banish an ancient horror from beyond the stars," Whisper added cheerfully. "Don't forget that part."

"Couldn't if we tried."

They stepped out into the storm together.

The rain hit immediately—cold needles against exposed skin, soaking through fabric in seconds. The wind whipped at them, carrying the smell of wet earth and distant lightning. Thunder rolled across the valley like a warning, felt as much as heard, vibrating in their chests. Lightning illuminated the distant silhouette of Thornhollow Castle, perched on its hill like a predator waiting, all sharp angles and malevolent towers against the roiling sky. Then darkness again.

But Theron didn't walk alone anymore.

Sera moved to his left, sword hand ready, her boots splashing through puddles forming on the road. Tomren took position on his right, crossbow up, scanning the darkness, rain streaming off the brim of his hood. Whisper clung to his shoulder, tiny claws digging in through wet fabric, warm despite the rain, breath tickling his neck.

This was it. No more planning. No more preparation. Just the long walk through the storm, toward the castle, toward answers, toward an ending.

One way or another.

The road to Thornhollow awaited.

Chapter 7: Into the Dark

The rain was relentless.

It hammered down in sheets, cold needles against their faces, turning the road to sucking mud that pulled at their boots with every step. The water soaked through robes within minutes—first the shoulders, then seeping down the back, the chest, until the fabric clung cold and heavy, chafing with every movement. Theron's hair was plastered to his forehead, water running into his eyes, down his neck in icy rivulets. But there was a strange comfort in it—the storm masked their approach, drowned their footsteps with its constant roar, blinded any watchers the witch might have posted.

Sera led the way, moving with a soldier's efficiency despite the mud sucking at her boots, each step making soft squelching sounds. Tomren brought up the rear, crossbow wrapped in oilcloth, eyes scanning the treeline through the rain-blurred darkness. Theron walked between them, one hand on his wand—the wood slick beneath his wet palm—the other shielding his face from the downpour. His legs ached from fighting the mud. His fingers were going numb.

Whisper had retreated deep into his hood, a small warm presence against his neck, fur damp despite the shelter.

"I hate rain," the familiar mutters.

"You're a rat. I thought rats liked sewers."

"That's a vicious stereotype."

Two Hours Later — The Edge of Thornhollow Forest

The trees rose before them like a wall of shadows, branches clawing at the stormy sky. Even in daylight, this forest would feel wrong—the branches twisted at unnatural angles, bark ridged and cracked like diseased skin, the undergrowth grew thick and grasping, thorns catching at their clothes. And there was a smell beneath the rain. Decay—not the clean rot of autumn leaves, but something putrid, like week-old meat left in the sun. Old magic, sharp and metallic on the tongue, making teeth ache. Something watching.

The temperature dropped as they approached. Not gradually—sharply. As if stepping across an invisible threshold into winter. One moment, rain-soaked and miserable. The next, freezing, the cold biting through wet clothes like knives. Theron's breath misted in white clouds. His wet fingers went numb around his wand, joints stiffening, the skin turning pale. His damp robes felt like ice pressed against his skin.

"Do you feel that?" he whispered. "The temperature drop—it's too sharp to be natural."

"Unnatural cold. Something's changed the air itself," Tomren said, voice low and steady.

"Dark magic," Whisper corrected from the hood. "Centuries of it, seeping into the land like poison into groundwater. The forest has been changed. It remembers every soul she's taken here."

Sera held up a fist. They stopped.

"Lost our first scout here," she said quietly, rain streaming down her scarred face. "Thirty paces in. Found the body seven days later, two miles out. No wounds. No marks." She paused. "No eyes."

"That's a grim find," Tomren said, his jaw tight.

"The forest itself isn't the danger," Whisper said from his hood. "It's her awareness. The trees, the animals, the shadows—they report to her. We need to move quickly and quietly."

Theron peered into the darkness between the trunks. His left thumb moved unconsciously across his fingertips—index, middle, ring, pinky—calculating risks, planning routes.

"Can you guide us around her watchpoints? Or are they too densely distributed?"

"Some of them. Not all." The familiar's voice is grim. "She's had centuries to weave her web. But there are... dead zones. Places where her attention doesn't reach. Old shrines. Cursed ground. Areas she's learned to avoid."

Sera exchanges a glance with Tomren. Her eyes swept the treeline—counting shadows, assessing angles, automatic as breathing. She rolled her shoulders once, a fighter's preparation.

"We follow the rat's lead, then."

The darkness under the canopy is absolute, pressing against their eyes like a physical weight. Rain and storm clouds have stolen what little daylight remained. The forest floor squelched beneath their boots, mud and rotting leaves releasing the smell of decomposition with every step. Water dripped from branches overhead, cold drops hitting necks and faces without warning.

Theron pulled the phosphorescent moss from his component pouch—Maren's gift. He crushed a small amount between his numb fingers, the moss releasing a faint earthy smell as it began to glow with a pale greenish light. The luminescence pulsed faintly, like a slow heartbeat. Not bright enough to carry far, but enough to see the twisted roots and dark hollows beneath their feet.

"Smart thinking," Sera whispered, watching the pale illumination. "Risk of detection?"

"Natural light," Whisper confirmed. "The forest is full of it. Her watchers won't notice one more patch of glowing fungus."

Whisper guided them through the forest like a thread through a needle. Left around the hollow oak—a raven nested there, one of hers. Right past the standing stones—she didn't look there anymore, not since something had driven her out decades ago. Straight through the stream, the water masking their scent.

Theron crushed more moss as needed, leaving faint glowing markers behind them—a trail back to safety, should they need it. His fingers found the component pouch at his belt, touching the silk bundle of Maren's gift with something like reverence.

Tomren's foot snaps a branch—the crack sharp and loud despite the rain, echoing through the unnatural silence.

They all freeze, hearts hammering, breath held.

Silence. Rain pattering on leaves. Thunder rumbling in the distance.

Then—movement in the canopy above. Something large, shifting between branches with a sound like leather scraping over wood. The sound of wings, leathery and wet, displacing air with heavy beats. A smell wafted down—carrion and sulfur, making Theron's stomach turn.

Theron's hand tightened on his wand, knuckles white, palm sweating despite the cold. He didn't look up. Looking up meant looking away from the path, losing his footing, making noise. His pulse thundered in his ears. Under his breath, barely audible over the pounding of his own heartbeat: "Praesidium... tenebris..." Half-formed incantation fragments, nervous energy seeking outlet.

The thing above them screamed—not a bird's cry, but something that sounded almost human. Almost. The sound vibrated through their chests, primal and wrong, making every instinct scream run.

Then it was gone, the sound of heavy wings fading into the storm, leaving only the smell of death behind.

"One of hers?" Sera breathed.

"One of many," Whisper confirmed, trembling. "Move. Faster."

One Hour Later — The Ravine

Theron emerged from the treeline onto familiar ground, boots sliding on wet stone at the ravine's edge. The ravine stretched before him, a dark gash in the earth, the river churning far below with the roar of swollen stormwater—white foam visible even in the darkness, the sound like continuous thunder. The spray reached up, misting their faces with icy droplets. And above, barely visible through the sheets of rain—

Thornhollow Castle.

It clung to the cliffside like a growth, like a cancer of stone, all twisted spires and dark stone that seemed to drink the light. Lightning illuminated it in brilliant flashes: a window here, reflecting white light like a dead eye; a battlement there, sharp against the storm; the great iron doors that he'd never enter through, studded and massive and forbidding.

But they weren't going in through the front. The air here tasted of stone and cold water and something else—the faint metallic tang of old blood, carried on the wind from the castle above.

"There." Theron pointed to the drainage grate—still broken, still hanging open. The river rushed past it, higher than before. "That's our entrance."

Sera studied the gap, fingers brushing the locket at her throat. "Water level's high. Half-flooded."

"It was dry when I escaped. The storm's filled it." Theron calculated, thumb moving across his fingertips again—measuring, weighing. "We'll be wading. Maybe swimming in places. But it's still passable."

"If it floods completely while we're inside?"

"Then we find another way out. Or we don't." He met her eyes. "This is the path I know. The path I survived."

She held his gaze for a long moment.

Then she nodded.

"Tomren. Get the rope ready."

The Descent

Tomren anchored the rope to a sturdy oak, testing it with his full weight—the hemp creaking but holding—and one by one, they descended into the ravine. The rocks were slick with rain, sharp edges hidden by moss and running water. More than once, Theron's foot slipped, his boot scraping against stone, hands burning as the rope slid through his palms before he caught himself. The muscles in his arms screamed. His shoulders ached. But the rope held, and his companions held, and somehow they reached the bottom without broken bones, landing in ankle-deep water that soaked immediately through his boots.

The river roared past, waist-high and cold—not the shocking cold of his first escape, but still brutal, stealing breath, making muscles cramp. The current tugged at their legs, hungry and strong. The drainage grate loomed ahead, a dark mouth in the cliff face, water pouring from it in a constant stream. Even from here, Theron could smell it—the rot, sweet and nauseating; the chemicals, sharp and acrid like burning copper; the wrongness that seeped from the castle's foundations. The smell made his eyes water.

This close to the castle, the air itself felt heavy. Oppressive. As if the stones above were watching, waiting, judging. The temperature had dropped again—colder than the forest, colder than should be possible. Their breath came in white clouds.

"Stay close to the walls," Theron shouted over the water. "Don't let the current take you. Maintain contact with the stone."

He entered first.

The Drainage Tunnels

The darkness swallowed them whole—complete, absolute, pressing against eyes and skin like a living thing.

Water surged around Theron's legs, thigh-deep and freezing, tugging at him with surprising strength. He pressed one hand against the slick stone wall, slime coating the surface, making his palm slide. Wand in the other hand, held high above the water. Behind him, Sera's breathing was steady, controlled despite the cold—he could hear the slight chattering of her teeth between breaths. Tomren cursed softly as he fought the current, the splash of his bulk displacing water echoing off close walls.

The smell down here was worse—not just rot, but chemical. Sharp and acrid, like burning copper mixed with sulfur, with something underneath that tasted bitter on the tongue. The residue of dark magic, washed down from whatever horrors happened in the castle above. The stench burned his nostrils, made his eyes water and his stomach heave. He breathed through his mouth, but that only made it worse—he could taste it.

"Whisper. Directions." His voice echoed strangely in the confined space.

"Straight for thirty paces. Then the first left—don't take the right, it's collapsed now. After that, up."

Theron pushed forward, step by step, muscles straining against the current. The water rose to his chest in places, so cold it made his ribs ache, made breathing difficult. The dark was absolute—he couldn't see his own hand in front of his face, could only feel the slimy wall beneath his palm, the icy water pulling at his robes. His foot touched something soft in the water—yielding, bloated, wrong—he didn't look down, didn't think about what it might be, just kept moving, skin crawling, trying not to imagine.

Then—a split in the tunnel.

"Left," Whisper confirmed.

Theron turned. The water was shallower here, only knee-deep. The tunnel began to slope upward. With each step, the chemical smell faded slightly, replaced by something else—old stone, torch smoke, and underneath it all, the faint metallic tang of blood.

"We're past the flood line now," Theron breathed. "Keep moving upward."

Ten Minutes Later — The Lower Dungeons

Theron emerged into a corridor he recognized, water streaming from his robes, forming puddles on the stone floor. Torchlight flickered on damp stone walls—the same enchanted flames that never died, casting dancing shadows. The cells lined both sides, iron bars rusted but solid, dark openings like mouths. The air here was warmer than the tunnels but still cold, still damp, smelling of mold and human waste and old fear.

His cell was there. Third on the left. The door still hung open, darkness beyond. Seeing it again made his chest tighten, memories flooding back—the cold, the hunger, the despair.

But they weren't alone.

A thrall stood at the far end of the corridor.

It had been human once. That was the worst part—the remnants of humanity still visible beneath what she'd done to it. It wore the tattered remains of a merchant's clothes, now filthy and torn, stained with things best not examined. Its skin had a gray pallor, like week-old corpse flesh, waxy and slack. The smell reached them even at this distance—rot and decay, the sweet-putrid stench of meat left too long. Its eyes...

Its eyes were empty. Not dead—worse than dead. Hollowed out. Whatever made this person a person was gone, consumed, leaving only the shell behind to serve. Looking into those eyes was like looking into an abyss.

The thrall swayed slightly, head tilted at an unnatural angle, as if listening to voices they couldn't hear. Its jaw hung slightly open, revealing teeth stained dark. It hadn't seen them yet, but its nostrils flared, sniffing the air.

Sera's hand moved to her sword, steel whispering against leather. Her eyes tracked the corridor—two exits, one alcove, no cover. Tomren silently raised his crossbow, bolt aimed center mass, and positioned himself at Sera's back without a word.

Whisper's claws dug into Theron's shoulder.

Two thralls. One visible, one hidden.

He hoped it wasn't.

Sneaking Past

She nodded.

Don't move. Don't breathe. Don't—

What did she do to you?

Chapter 8: The Study

Theron took the center path, climbing another narrow staircase that groaned beneath his weight, wood creaking with each step, centuries-old timbers protesting. Because the first portrait showed a child.

Ten years old. Still human.

Accepted.

Maren arrived within a week of the witch's defeat, three wagons creaking under the weight of supplies—dried herbs bundled in sacks that filled the air with the sharp smell of thyme and sage, glass vials wrapped in straw that clinked softly with every bump, ceramic mortars and pestles packed in wool. And a sharp tongue that hadn't dulled with age.

"Someone needs to manage the medicinal wing," she said gently but firmly, white hair escaping from its practical bun as always, "and without guidance, you'd all be poisoning yourselves with mislabeled tinctures within a month."

But later, after the supplies were unloaded and the apprentices dismissed, she sat with Theron in the evening light—golden through the cleaned windows, warm on their faces—and told him the real reason. The leather journal sat between them on the table, worn and familiar.

"Thirty years," she said, voice rough with decades of grief, fingers tracing the journal's binding. "Thirty years I kept that journal, like tending a garden in the dark. Wrote down every name I could find, every person who vanished that no one else remembered. I told myself it was enough—that remembering was enough."

She looked at her hands, weathered and stained from years of grinding herbs and mixing tinctures, the lines deep, the knuckles swollen slightly with age. "But it wasn't. It was just... bearable. Like water wearing at stone. Lily deserved more than a name in a hidden journal. She deserved to be avenged."

"The witch is dead," Theron said carefully. "You have justice, at least. That has to count for something."

"I do. And it's not enough either." Maren's eyes were bright, but no tears fell—she'd cried them all out long ago. "But this—training healers who'll actually help people, teaching ethical magic, making sure no one else gets taken? That's not vengeance. That's not just remembering. It's like planting a tree where something was burned away."

"That's meaning," Theron finished softly.

She nodded. "Lily wanted to be a healer. Wanted to help people, the way water helps seeds grow. She never got the chance. But I can make sure others do. I can teach them what she would have taught." A pause. "It won't bring her back. But it makes her matter. Makes her loss count for something. Makes new life from old grief."

She now ran the herbalist division, training apprentices in the art of ethical potion-making and natural magic. Her sister's name was carved above the entrance to the greenhouse—remembered at last.

Some days Theron saw her standing there, fingers tracing the carved letters. Lips moving in silent conversation with someone who disappeared three decades ago.

The Memorial Wall

On the eastern courtyard wall, carved into stone, were the names. Every victim Maren could find record of—in journals, ledgers, transaction tables, and her own thirty years of careful documentation. Every person the witch's magic tried to erase from memory. The letters were deep, painstakingly carved, filled with gold leaf that caught the sunlight.

The list grew as they discovered more. Theron helped her add new ones, chiseling letters into stone with steady hands and quiet reverence, the tap-tap-tap of hammer on chisel echoing in the courtyard. Stone dust coated his fingers, settling into the grooves of his palms.

Lily of Millhaven, Healer
Lucian Ashborne, Scholar
Elena Greytower, age 14
Marcus Thane, Merchant
The Carpenter's Daughter (name lost)
Brother Aldous, Monk
Sarah, Sarah, Sarah—three different women, three different years

The names went on. Dozens of them. Some had families and occupations carved beneath. Some had only a first name, their surnames lost to time and enchantment. Some were marked simply: Victim, name unknown. But all were remembered now. All were permanent. All mattered.

Students passed it every day on their way to the greenhouses, boots scuffing on cobblestones. Visitors stopped and read, fingers tracing the carved letters, lips moving silently through the list, breath catching on names they recognized or didn't. A reminder of what happened when power corrupted without check. A promise that these deaths would not be forgotten, would not be allowed to be forgotten.

Theron ran his fingers over Lucian's name sometimes, feeling the carved letters beneath his touch—the grooves deep, the gold leaf smooth and warm in the sun. Real. Permanent. Remembered.

And below the names, in larger script, a dedication:

"No one is forgotten. No one is alone.
This Institute stands so no more names need be carved."

Lucian's Memorial

In a quiet corner of the Institute's grounds, beneath an ancient oak whose branches spread wide overhead, stood a simple memorial stone. The grass around it was always trimmed, the ground clear of fallen leaves. The inscription read:

LUCIAN ASHBORNE
Scholar, Seeker, Son
"His curiosity lit the way for others"

Theron visited every morning before dawn, when the grounds were empty and quiet, when dew still clung to the grass and soaked through his boots. The air smelled of earth and growing things, cool and fresh. He told Lucian about the students—their questions, their discoveries, their caution. He told him about Whisper's latest sarcasm, about Sera's tactical drills, about Elina's progress. His voice was quiet, hoarse with early morning, but steady.

His thumb rubbed absently against his fingers as he spoke—the old thinking gesture, softer now without the weight of immediate crisis. Just habit. Just comfort.

He never managed to recover Lucian's remains from the oubliette—the collapse during the final battle sealed it forever. But he had the book Lucian was translating, displayed in a place of honor in the library. He had Lucian's notes, preserved under glass. He had his son's handwriting, his questions, his brilliant, dangerous curiosity.

And now he taught others what Lucian never learned: that knowledge without wisdom was a blade that cut the wielder. That some doors, once opened, could not be closed. That seeking truth required knowing which truths were safe to seek.

"I'm sorry I didn't find you in time," Theron whispers to the stone. "But I promise—no one else will fall the way you did. Not here. Not while I'm watching."

In his pocket, he carried the folded journal page. Always. His other hand checked his component pouch—full now, ingredients neatly organized, each compartment stocked. The weight of it was different than before. Not desperate scarcity, but careful preparation.

Sera stayed longer than she planned.

"Just until Elina recovers," she said at first. Then: "Just until the perimeter defenses are established." Then she stopped making excuses.

The truth came out one evening, three weeks after the battle. She and Theron sat in the reconstructed study, going over security plans. She'd been quiet all day—the kind of quiet that meant she was working something out. Her fingers brushed her locket once, then stilled as she gathered her thoughts.

"Can't go back," she said finally.

Theron looked up from the maps. "To what?"

"Mercenary work. Contract hunting. Moving town to town taking coin for kills." She traced the edge of the table with one finger. "Six months tracking leads. Following rumors. Bribing informants. Six months of zero results. No kingdom sent soldiers. No guild sent wizards. Everyone knew about the Witch of Thornhollow. Everyone looked the other way."

Her jaw tightened. "Had to hire myself to my own damn rescue mission. Pay Tomren with coin I didn't have. Risk everything because no one else would."

"You saved her," Theron said, choosing his words carefully.

"We saved her. And you know what that taught me?" Sera leaned forward, intensity burning in her eyes. "System doesn't work. People disappear. No one comes. Want protection? Build it yourself."

She gestured at the castle around them. "This place? Not just about teaching magic. About creating something that responds. Next witch rises, next demon cult takes root, next village screams for help—we'll hear them. We'll come."

"Is that why you're drilling the guards so hard?"

A fierce smile. "Building a response team, Theron. Small. But ours. People who know what it costs to lose someone. Who'll never look the other way."

She commanded the Institute's guard now—a mix of former soldiers, reformed cultists seeking redemption, and locals who wanted to protect what was being built. Tomren served as her second, his crossbow now mounted above the barracks door, a symbol of the battle that started everything.

During morning drills, Sera moved through the courtyard with tactical precision—eyes tracking exits, blind spots, defensive positions. The habit never left. But her shoulders would roll occasionally, releasing tension that no longer came from immediate threat. Her hand adjusted her sword belt—once, twice—not from nerves, but from the simple comfort of knowing it was there.

But on quiet nights, Theron still found her in Elina's room, watching her daughter sleep. Making sure she was real. Making sure she was breathing. Her hand would drift to her locket—the familiar weight of it—then drop away. The gesture was quieter now, less frantic. Not panic. Just... remembering.

Tomren found something he didn't expect: purpose beyond a debt.

For fifteen years, he'd followed Sera because he owed her his life. It was simple, clean—a soldier's oath, a life for a life. When they rescued Elina, when the witch fell, he'd expected that to be the end. Debt paid. Contract fulfilled.

"You're free to go," Sera told him, a week after the battle. "Release you from the oath. Don't owe me anything anymore."

He'd looked at her like she was speaking a foreign language.

"Where exactly would I go?"

"Anywhere. Home. Another company. Retirement." She'd smiled, trying to make it light. "Earned it."

"Home burned fifteen years ago. No other company to speak of. And retirement sounds boring as hell." He'd crossed his arms, stubborn as always. "You released me from the debt. Can't release me from giving a damn."

Now he trained the new guards—many of them younger than Elina, farm boys and merchant daughters who wanted to protect their homes. He was patient in a way that surprised him, firm but fair. The crossbow above the barracks door wasn't his anymore—he had a new one, better balanced, with sights he calibrated himself.

He maintained it every evening, the familiar click-click-click of the mechanism a soothing rhythm. Sometimes he hummed while he worked—old marching songs from campaigns he barely remembered. His positioning was still protective, still tactical, but now it came from choice rather than debt. He'd stand by the gate during student arrivals, or near Elina's window during her lessons, not hovering but present.

But the old one stayed up there. A reminder of the day he decided to choose his purpose instead of just fulfilling an obligation.

Some nights he joined Sera on her vigil outside Elina's room. Not speaking, just standing guard. The debt was paid. But the duty remained. Not because he had to.

Because he wanted to.

Elina studied magic.

She had talent—raw, untrained, but real. The witch kept her for a reason, Theron realized. Her blood carried something ancient, something resonant with old magic. Morganna could sense it, taste it in the ritual preparations. Planned to harvest that potential, to feed it to Szeth-Kaan as bait for a proper host.

Instead, it would be nurtured.

But recovery wasn't linear.

Some days Elina was the brilliant, sharp-tongued girl who bargained with a wizard while half-conscious. She asked cutting questions, challenged his teaching, laughed at Whisper's jokes, her eyes bright and engaged. She was fearless in the laboratory, cautious but curious, hands steady as she measured components, voice clear as she recited incantations.

Other days she couldn't leave her room. Couldn't stand anyone touching her, flinching away from even gentle hands. Flinched at shadows in doorways, breath catching, fingers trembling. Sera sat with her on those days, not speaking, just present, back against the wall where Elina could see her, close enough to reach but not touching. Sometimes Elina talked about the six months—the cold stone against her skin, the ritual marks being carved with needles that burned, the witch's voice promising it would be over soon, would stop hurting soon, just a little more. Sometimes she just stared at the wall, rocking slightly, humming that tuneless melody she used to keep herself sane, the sound thin and fragile in the quiet room.

"Does it get better?" she asked Theron once, after a particularly bad night.

He thought about lying. About offering false comfort.

"Let me think..." He paused, rubbing his thumb against his fingers. "Yes. But not in a straight line—recovery follows more of a... a spiral pattern. And not all the way. Some scars don't fade. The psychological literature on trauma suggests—" He stopped himself. "Sorry. Yes. It gets better. But slowly."

She nodded, like she'd expected that answer. Like she'd needed the truth more than the comfort.

"Good. I don't want to forget. I just want it to stop defining me."

She called him "Master" sometimes, which he hated. He told her to use his name. She grinned and did it anyway. On good days, it was teasing. On bad days, it was armor—formality keeping the world at a safe distance.

When Theron taught her, his thumb would rub against his fingers as he considered her questions. "Good question," he'd say, reaching for his component pouch to demonstrate a principle. "Let me think about how to explain this... the key principle is to question everything. That's how you stay safe—through constant verification and skepticism. Does that make sense?" The pouch was always full now—sulfur, copper wire, quicksilver, bat guano—everything organized and ready. Not the desperate scraps of a wizard escaping a dungeon, but the careful preparation of a teacher who knew the cost of being caught unprepared.

She was seventeen now. Too young to have survived what she did. Too old to pretend it didn't happen.

The Thralls—those who could be saved—recovered slowly. The witch's hold was magical, not physical, and with her death, it began to fade. Some regained themselves within days. Others took weeks. A few...

A few would never fully return. But they were cared for, given purpose, treated with dignity. The hollow-eyed man from the tower tended the gardens now. He didn't speak, but he smiled sometimes, when the flowers bloomed.

Whisper remained.

He had his own quarters—a converted dovecote with soft bedding, a private cheese supply, and a window overlooking the valley. He advised, he taught, he occasionally insulted visiting dignitaries with centuries-old wit.

But mostly, he stayed close to him.

"You could leave," Theron told him once. "The contract doesn't bind you anymore. You're free. If you wanted to go—"

Whisper looked at him with those ancient, knowing eyes.

"I am free," he said. "Free to choose. And I choose this. I choose you."

He'd never mentioned cheese since. Theron brought it anyway.

Theron didn't sleep well.

The others thought he was working late—and he was, often. But the real reason he stayed up, reading by candlelight in the converted study, the flame guttering and wavering as hours passed, was simpler: he was afraid of the dreams.

Morganna's memories didn't fade when he absorbed her power. They integrated. He remembered things he never experienced—the smell of smoke as she burned, acrid and choking; the faces of the seven friends who betrayed her, their laughter echoing; the first time she tasted human life force and felt the addictive rush of stolen years, like lightning in her veins. The memories felt like his own. Tasted like his own. That was the horrifying part.

He remembered murdering those friends. Felt the satisfaction of their terror, their pleading, their suffering. The righteousness of her revenge burning bright and clean. And some nights, some awful nights, he understood it. Agreed with it. Felt his hands itching to reach for that power again, to use it, to make someone else suffer the way he'd suffered—

"Still awake?" Whisper asked one night, appearing silently in the study, a shadow moving through shadows.

"Can't sleep. It's been three hours now. Maybe four." His voice was rough, tired. His eyes burned from staring at parchment by candlelight.

"Her memories?"

Theron nodded, not trusting his voice. His hands trembled slightly, resting on the desk.

Whisper settled on the desk, ancient eyes reflecting candlelight. "The fact that you're afraid of becoming like her is proof you won't. She never questioned herself. Never doubted. Never saw her reflection and felt horror at what she'd become."

"I have her power, Whisper. Every day I can feel it—centuries of stolen life, compressed into me. Sometimes I cast a simple spell and the output efficiency is... it's like I'm twenty wizards at once. The temptation to use it, to really use it, to test the upper limits of what's theoretically possible—"

"Is why you established rules for yourself," Whisper interrupted. "Why you require oversight from Maren and Sera on any major working. Why you teach others so you're never the only one holding knowledge. Why you refuse to make the same mistakes she did."

"What if—let me think—what if it's not enough? What if the protocols fail?"

"Then your friends will stop you. That's what they're for." Whisper's voice softened. "You're not alone in a dungeon, Theron. You're surrounded by people who care whether you live or die, who'll call you out if you cross a line. She isolated herself. You're building a community. That's the difference."

Theron still didn't sleep well. But he slept better than he did that first month, when every dream was fire and betrayal and the seductive pull of dark magic.

The absorbed power was a tool, he reminded himself. Dangerous, like any tool. But tools could build.

As long as he never forgot what it cost.

The Future:

Word spread. Slowly at first, then faster.

A place where knowledge was shared, not hoarded. Where wizards and herbalists and scientists worked together instead of in jealous isolation. Where the dark history of Thornhollow was studied so it could never be repeated.

They came from everywhere—the curious, the brilliant, the broken, the hopeful. He turned no one away, though he watched carefully. Power corrupted. He knew that better than anyone now.

In the lecture hall, Theron taught with his thumb rubbing against his fingers as he explained complex theory. "Let me think... transmutation requires balance," he'd say, the gesture punctuating his words. "The fundamental principle being that energy cannot be created or destroyed—only transformed. Does that make sense? Any questions on the theoretical framework?" His hand would check his component pouch between demonstrations, an unconscious reassurance that everything he needed was there. Full. Ready. No longer scraping together scraps in desperation.

But power also built. Created. Protected.

It wasn't perfect. Nothing built from ruins ever was.

Two apprentices had to be expelled for attempting forbidden rituals in the old dungeons. Three families pulled their children out after learning the Institute's director was a "dark wizard who murdered the Witch." A merchant guild in Millhaven refused to trade with them, claiming they were "dabbling in evil."

Sera handled the security responses—always positioning herself between the threat and the students, her hand adjusting her sword belt in that familiar way. Not nervousness. Readiness. The tactical assessment never stopped, but it no longer consumed her.

Funding was precarious. The salvaged gold from Morganna's hoard helped, but it wouldn't last forever. They needed patrons, donors, support from kingdoms that still viewed Thornhollow with suspicion.

Some of the reformed cultists relapsed. One fled in the night, stealing supplies. Another tried to continue the witch's work in secret until Sera's guards caught him. Trust was hard to build and easy to shatter.

Tomren had been on duty that night—positioned at his usual overlook, crossbow within reach, humming to himself while he watched the grounds. The click of his maintenance work had stopped the moment he spotted movement. Old habits, new purpose.

And the nightmares weren't just Theron's. Half the residents woke screaming some nights. The thralls who recovered carried scars that didn't show on skin. Elina wasn't the only one who had bad days.

But they kept building anyway.

Because the alternative—giving up, walking away, letting Thornhollow return to being a cursed name whispered in fear—was worse.

Theron stood at his study window, morning light warm on his face, watching a new group of students arrive through the gates. Young faces, eager faces, full of potential they didn't yet understand. Voices carrying up from the courtyard—excited, nervous, hopeful. The smell of bread drifted from the kitchens. Someone was singing in the greenhouse. The sound of hammers from the expansion construction echoed off stone.

Below in the courtyard, Sera assessed the new arrivals with trained eyes—scanning for threats that weren't there, hand resting near her sword hilt out of habit, positioning herself near the gate where she could see all approaches. Her shoulders rolled once—settling weight, releasing tension. Tomren stood at his usual post, crossbow maintenance tools at his belt, the familiar click-click of the mechanism carrying faintly, humming something tuneless as he observed the crowd. His positioning was protective, angled to cover Sera's back without hovering.

Theron's fingers found his component pouch again—full, always full now, the weight of it comforting and familiar—and he allowed himself a small moment of gratitude. Not for the power he'd absorbed. For the peace to use it carefully. For friends who would stop him if he went too far. For a purpose beyond survival.

Whisper settled on his shoulder, warm weight, familiar presence.

"Nervous?"

"Always. With this many new variables, the probability of—"

"Good. Complacency is how witches are made."

Theron smiled, touching the spellbook at his chest—warm leather against his palm, Lucian's legacy, now his to honor and continue.

"Then let's make sure we do better. Much better."

The morning sun continued to rise over Thornhollow, light chasing away shadows, warming stone that had been cold for too long. Below, the students passed through the gates, beginning their journey toward knowledge tempered with wisdom, power balanced with responsibility.

And above them all, carved in stone where everyone could see: No one is forgotten. No one is alone.
